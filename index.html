The dormant subspace, waking up for touch frozen critical (vision) trainable dormant (touch)
Abstract

A zero-sum trade-off, resolved

Touch supplies the physical grounding — friction, compliance, hardness — that vision alone often cannot resolve. Giving a multimodal large language model this sense is a natural step toward reasoning about the physical world.

But on compact, edge-deployable models the parameter budget is tight, and naively learning touch overwrites the vision-language knowledge the model already has. Splash scores how important every pretrained weight is, splits the network into a critical subspace and a dormant one, then freezes the critical weights as a stable anchor and updates only the dormant subspace to internalize touch. The result is non-destructive modality expansion: tactile reasoning is gained, vision-language reasoning is kept, and inference cost in the LLM stays the same.

The problem

Learning touch shouldn't cost you vision

When a small MLLM is tuned on a little tactile data, it can start hallucinating about what it sees. Below, the same model describes an image correctly (top), then — after a naive tactile branch is added — invents a "flexible rubbery tool" and an "open" can that aren't there. That's catastrophic forgetting in the visual domain.

Contributions

Three ideas behind Splash

01

Parameter isolation

A visual-relative importance metric finds a dormant subspace and confines all tactile updates to it — zero added inference overhead in the LLM, and forgetting is held off by construction.

02

Single-stage training

Frozen critical weights act as stable vision-language anchors, so the tactile front-end and dormant weights train together in one pass — no multi-stage alignment pipeline.

03

Works at small scale

On Qwen2.5-VL-3B and InternVL-1B, Splash reaches state-of-the-art tactile reasoning while preserving general-purpose ability — outperforming prior 7B-scale methods.

How it works

Locate the dormant subspace, then wake it up

STEP 1 — LOCATE

Score every weight against vision

Using a small vision-language calibration set, each weight gets an importance score from its magnitude and input activation. Low-scoring weights — the dormant subspace — contribute little to pretrained behavior. The first and last transformer blocks are always kept critical for stability.

STEP 2 — ALIGN

Train only the dormant weights for touch

A binary mask sends gradients exclusively to the dormant subspace while the tactile front-end trains freely. Critical vision-language pathways are left untouched — unlike additive methods such as LoRA, there's no perturbation merged back into the backbone.

Results

State-of-the-art touch, vision intact

Qualitative

What Splash feels

Given a paired RGB image and a tactile contact image, each model predicts surface attributes. Splash stays grounded in touch where baselines fall back on visual appearance — calling a coarse surface "smooth" or "reflective."
